# Citing and labelling AI

*Section: Study organisation · Language: en · Source: https://studieren.univie.ac.at/en/using-ai-in-your-studies/academic-work-and-writing-with-ai/citing-and-labelling-ai/*

In order to ensure the visibility of your independent contribution, it is essential to present your work transparently and in a manner that allows for clear and accurate tracking of the use of AI. It is crucial to clearly distinguish the parts of the work that originate from you, ensuring that your independent performance is accurately assessed. Failure to disclose the use of AI could be considered a breach of the principles of good academic practice.

The responsibility for determining how the use of AI is to be made transparent lies with the respective lecturer, the study programme director (SPL) or the respective department. In the absence of specific requirements, established best practices may serve as a useful guideline.

A distinction is made between references within the text and entries in the bibliography and, secondly, between content generated entirely by AI and content produced with the assistance of AI.

## References within the text

With regard to references within the text, a distinction is made between the literal reproduction of AI-generated content and the collaborative development of content with the help of AI.

#### Literal reproduction

If AI-generated content is incorporated into the text verbatim, this may be treated as a conventional quotation. Traceability is ensured if, in addition to the quoted text,it is recommended to indicate

- the name of the AI-tool and, where applicable, the company behind it
- the model used
- the year of use
- and the prompt that generated the text

Example:

Text generated by ChatGPT (OpenAI, model o1, 2024. Prompt: “Explain the fundamental principles of good academic practice in one sentence.”)

In this case, the prompt  documents your contribution by indicating which instruction was given.

In practice, however, AI output often is created in an iterative process involving multiple prompts and queries.

#### Collaborative development

Complete documentation of all individual AI interactions is generally not practical. What is more important is that it is clear how AI was used in principle and that the writer was responsible for controlling, selecting and revising the content.

The documentation can, for example, be presented in a table and document page by page which AI tool was used and how. Alternatively, a single reference to collaborative AI use, e.g. in the introduction or in the methods section, may be sufficient to create transparency without disproportionately increasing the documentation effort. The crucial factor is that your own contribution is clearly recognisable and the results remain scientifically verifiable.

Example:

In the preparation of this thesis, AI-supported applications were used as auxiliary tools. ChatGPT (OpenAI) was employed to develop linguistic variants, revise draft texts stylistically, refine formulations, support the structure of individual sections and improve the overall readability of passages. In addition, it provided assistance with Excel, for example by suggesting suitable formulae for the quantitative analysis of contributions and categories. ChatGPT was also used to produce draft translations of Italian and French texts and to convert text from non-machine-readable scans into editable and searchable formats. DeepL was used in addition, both for translations and for the linguistic optimisation of translation drafts produced with ChatGPT, thereby further improving the accuracy and stylistic quality of the texts. Finally, Transkribus was used for the transcription of historical documents written in Kurrent script. All of the aforementioned applications were used exclusively in a supportive capacity and in agreement with the thesis supervisor. Their use was in accordance with the applicable AI guidelines of the University of Vienna and with due regard for academic independence: they served to facilitate linguistic, technical and practical work steps and did not replace independent academic work. All results constituted solely technical or linguistic support and were critically reviewed, adapted and contextualised by the author. The selection, analysis and interpretation of sources, the development of the argument, the substantive evaluation of the data and the final drafting of the text were carried out independently. The author bears sole responsibility for all content, translations and results of this thesis. The use of AI-supported tools is therefore to be understood not as a delegation of tasks, but as a reflective complement within a collaborative working process.

In particular in the case of theses, be sure to clarify with your supervisor how the use of AI is to be documented.

## References in the bibliography

AI tools are not considered traditional sources, as interaction with them, similar to personal conversations, is not reproducible or openly accessible.

Reference only with publicly accessible link

According to international citation standards, AI tools should therefore only be included in a bibliography or reference list if a public URL to the respective chat can be provided. Although tools such as ChatGPT generate unique URLs for each conversation, these are generally accessible only with the user’s login credentials and are therefore not available to third parties ([The Chicago Manual of Stile Online](https://www.chicagomanualofstyle.org/qanda/data/faq/topics/Documentation/faq0422.html)).
