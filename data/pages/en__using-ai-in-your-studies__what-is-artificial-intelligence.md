# What is Artificial Intelligence?

*Section: Study organisation · Language: en · Source: https://studieren.univie.ac.at/en/using-ai-in-your-studies/what-is-artificial-intelligence/*

Artificial intelligence refers to computer-based systems that technically simulate certain human cognitive and linguistic abilities. These systems can generate texts, images or other content, but they do not understand these themselves and do not produce knowledge of their own. The use of AI systems also entails risks.

For degree programmes and academic work, this means that AI can provide support, but it does not replace independent thinking, critical evaluation or personal responsibility.

#

## What is artificial intelligence?

Artificial intelligence is commonly understood to refer to computer-based systems that can perform tasks typically requiring human intelligence. These include pattern recognition, language processing, decision-making and adapting to new situations. However, such systems are not truly "intelligent" in the sense in which intelligence is generally understood. They generate output solely on the basis of probabilities derived from linguistic patterns. This means:

- AI systems do not understand content, but calculate probabilities.
- They cannot make moral or ethical judgements.
- They do not possess emotional or social intelligence.

Therefore, while they may imitate human intelligence, they do not possess it themselves.

## How do AI systems work?

Modern AI systems work on the basis of data. They analyse large amounts of sample data and learn statistical correlations from it. On this basis, they make predictions or generate results that appear meaningful or plausible at first glance.

### Human in the Loop

Responsibility for the use, interpretation and evaluation of AI results therefore always lies with humans.

### Two basic types of AI models

In artificial intelligence, there are two basic types of models, to put it simply:

- Discriminative AI recognises patterns in existing data and assigns new inputs to known categories or values. Typical applications include spam filters and image recognition. Such systems learn to recognise distinguishing features and make decisions based on existing classes.
- Generative AI goes one step further: it generates new content by combining patterns, structures and probabilities from training data. This results in texts, images, music or videos that can appear to be human-created content.

Generative AI models have been "trained" with large amounts of data. This means that they have derived patterns, structures and statistical relationships between words from a vast amount of data, which they use to generate new data on this basis. Depending on the type of data that generative models produce, a distinction is made between language models, image models, audio models and video models, for example.

### Large Language Models (LLMs)

A widespread form of generative AI are large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT, which specialise in processing human language. They can generate text by calculating which word or sentence fragment is most likely to follow next in a given context.

This means that an LLM has no consciousness and no real understanding of content. In general, it only answers this question:**„Based on the probabilites I have learned, what is the most likely continuation of this text?**

The model therefore does not recognise "meaning" in the human sense, but only:

- statistical correlations between words
- typical sentence structures
- typical argumentation patterns
- typical answers to typical questions

The model does not understand, but the learned patterns and probabilities are so good that the result appears to be understanding.

This does not generate any new knowledge: LLMs combine existing linguistic patterns to create new texts. They do not understand content, reflect on it critically or conduct their own research. Their output is based solely on statistical correlations between words.

## Risks associated with the use of AI

The use of AI tools in academic studies also carries risks based on how large language models work.

### Hallucinations

Sometimes generative AI models produce texts that sound very plausible but are factually incorrect. It hallucinates. This happens because the model

- does not have a genuine internally stored database
- does not distinguish between "sounds plausible" and "is verifiably true"

The system's sole function is to optimise responses to prompts by identifying the most linguistcally probable response.

Example:

If you request a source or quotation and the model has seen many responses during training that follow the pattern:  „The study by XY (2021) shows that ...", then it constructs something along these lines, even if the specific study has never existed.

### Bias and misinterpretations

AI models are designed to learn the probabilities on which their answers are based from the training data to which they are exposed. Should the data in question contain

- perspectives that are overrepresented,
- groups that are portrayed stereotypically,
- or terms that frequently appear in the same context,

the model will, then, "adopt" these patterns without realising that they are problematic.

Example**:**

If the term "professor" is frequently associated with male forms/examples in the training data, the model is more likely to interpret the term in the same way.

This has implications for the reliability of the results. While AI-generated texts can appear linguistically convincing, they may contain factual errors or reproduce biases from the training data. Large Language models are unable to assess the accuracy of their statements, as they lack the understanding to comprehend content and do not access a verifiable collection of knowledge. They have just learned patterns from large amounts of text during training.

### What implications does this have for the use of AI in my Studies?

- It is important to note that generarive tools such as ChatGPT are not intended for use as research tools and should not be relied upon as reliable sources of knowledge.
- They cannot substitute specialist literature and may also provide incorrect and inaccurate information.
- They may continue, rephrase or summarise texts, but there is no guarantee that they are factually correct.
- Risk of plagiarism: Due to the way AI tools generate text, existing content may be reproduced without alteration or only slightly modified without citing sources.

It is imperative that all statements made with the assistance of AI are meticulously reviewed and verified using reliable sources. Students must assume full responsibility for the academic integrity of their work, including its content, arguments and results.
