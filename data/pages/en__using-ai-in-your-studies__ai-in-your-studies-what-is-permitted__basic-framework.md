# Basic framework for AI use

*Section: Study organisation · Language: en · Source: https://studieren.univie.ac.at/en/using-ai-in-your-studies/ai-in-your-studies-what-is-permitted/basic-framework/*

The use of AI in degree programmes is not generally prohibited, but nor is it permitted without any restrictions. Whether and how you may use AI always depends on the specific context, such as:

- the course type
- the examination or assessment format
- the specific requirements set by the lecturer
- the respective learning objectives
- the [principles of good academic practice](/en/using-ai-in-your-studies/academic-work-and-writing-with-ai/good-scientific-practice/#c1269035)

As a general rule, you are responsible for all work that you submit, including any that has been created using AI tools. This implies that:

- You are responsible for the accuracy, quality and traceability of your work.
- Any incorrect statements, distortions or fabricated sources are your responsibility.
- While AI may support you in your work, the substantive contribution and independent thinking must be your own.

## Learning outcomes and independence

In degree programmes at the University of Vienna, courses and examinations pursue clearly defined learning outcomes. These are designed to develop subject-specific competences and to foster academic work. This includes understanding and accurately presenting content, applying knowledge to one’s own research questions and developing independent arguments, structuring texts coherently and using sources appropriately.

The knowledge and skills required to achieve the learning outcomes must be developed independently. If AI assumes these tasks, the learning outcomes cannot be achieved. The competences associated with the learning outcomes are demonstrated in various course and examination formats, for example in examinations, presentations or written assignments. These competences must be demonstrated independently. This means that your work must reflect your own reasoning and learning processes.

This also determines how AI may generally be used in degree programmes:

- It may support learning processes, for example when structuring ideas or revising language.
- However, it must not assume those competences that are to be assessed within the framework of a course or examination.

Wie KI-Tools genutzt werden können, hängt also maßgeblich vom jeweiligen Lernziel ab, und diese können sich von Lehrveranstaltung zu Lehrveranstaltung unterscheiden.

How AI tools may be used therefore depends to a significant extent on the respective learning outcome, which may vary from course to course.

Example:

- When translating a passage from French in an academic paper on a specific topic, it is important to engage with the concrete content. In this case, the use of an AI translation tool may be unproblematic, provided that the translation is checked for accuracy and the use of AI is disclosed.
- In a language degree programme, translation itself is a central learning outcome. Therefore, if a translation task is delegated to an AI tool in this context, the independent performance expected of students would be undermined.

If AI performs the work on your behalf, this contravenes the principles of academic integrity. Whether you are permitted to use artificial intelligence in your degree programme therefore primarily depends on whether the required learning outcomes and your independent contribution are preserved.

### In short:

Although AI can provide support, it is important to remember that it should not be used to replace the work that is expected of you.

This recommendation can be put into practice by configuring an AI tool in a way that that the support it provides is aligned with the respective learning outcomes. For this purpose, an [AI tutor](/en/using-ai-in-your-studies/academic-work-and-writing-with-ai/ai-tutor-for-academic-writing/) can be developed that offers support while at the same time helping to safeguard independent work.
